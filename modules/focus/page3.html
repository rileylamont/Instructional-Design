Mindfulness

Mindfulness is the ability to become aware of your thoughts and emotions in the present moment without judgment. It’s not about clearing your mind—it’s about recognizing where your mind is and guiding it back.

Decades of research show that mindfulness improves focus, emotion regulation, and even the structure of your brain’s prefrontal cortex—the center of executive function.

And the good news? You’ve already begun practicing mindfulness in earlier modules when you increased awareness of your thoughts and emotions. Now, we’ll take that awareness and turn it into a tool to regain your focus.

Here’s how to practice:

  • Sit comfortably. Let your body be still and relaxed.
  • Remove external distractions. Silence your phone, turn off any background noise.
  • Focus on your breath. Inhale slowly. Exhale gently. Let your attention rest here.
  • Observe your thoughts. As thoughts and emotions arise, just watch them. Don’t react or judge.
  • Sit with discomfort. Sometimes, you might feel more anxious or distracted at first. That’s okay. Stay with it. Let the storm pass.
  • Be patient. After a few minutes, your mental momentum will begin to slow down. Try 10 minutes and adjust based on your needs.

It’s normal to get caught up in a thought and forget you're trying to focus. The moment you notice your mind has wandered—that’s progress! That means your attention is improving.

Action

Once your internal distractions have settled, even if it’s not perfect, it’s time to move. Don’t wait for perfect focus or motivation. Just start.

Focus is like a muscle: it grows through repetition. Practicing mindfulness isn’t about achieving stillness. It’s about building the ability to return to your focus, over and over again.

The goal isn’t perfect focus—it’s intentional redirection.

Remember: the rocket ship doesn’t stop all at once. But with the right effort in the right direction, it does change course.

And so can you.
