Pride

Strategies

Here are strategies to help you zero in on your target.

1. Overcoming Avoidance

Avoidance is part of the behavioral avoidance cycle:
Task makes you uncomfortable → You avoid it → You feel temporary relief → Avoidance is reinforced.

  • Insecure pride avoids because it fears failure, discomfort, or being exposed.
  • Arrogant pride avoids by pretending the task isn’t worth its time or is beneath them.

Break the loop:

  • Use exposure: do the task anyway, even if it’s uncomfortable or feels “beneath you.”
  • Name the emotion: is it fear of failure (insecurity) or fear of looking imperfect (arrogance)?
  • Reflect: “What’s the worst that could happen—and what if this task teaches me something I don’t yet know?”

2. Reversing Helplessness

Helplessness is often learned—and that means it can be unlearned.

  • Insecure pride says: “I can’t do this. It’s not who I am.”
  • Arrogant pride says: “This isn’t my fault. I’m already doing everything right.”

Steps:

  • Acknowledge both kinds of false beliefs: either “I’m powerless” or “I’m blameless.”
  • Focus on small wins: confidence comes from effort, not ego.
  • Repeat affirming truths:
    • “I can do hard things.”
    • “I can grow beyond this, even if I don’t want to admit I need to.”

3. Challenging False Identity

Outdated self-labels are not facts—they're schemas: mental shortcuts built from past experience or pride-protective narratives.

  • Insecure pride says: “I’m just not good at this.”
  • Arrogant pride says: “That’s not my problem—I don’t need to change.”

Reframe the story:

  • Notice identity-based statements (“I’m not a morning person” or “That’s just who I am”).
  • Ask: “Is this a fact, or a defense I’ve adopted?”
  • Replace it with:
    • “I’m capable of learning.”
    • “Maybe I don’t know everything yet—and that’s okay.”

4. Increasing Self-Awareness

Pride lives in blind spots. The more you can observe your inner world, the less power your ego has to sabotage you.

  • Insecure pride hides behind doubt.
  • Arrogant pride hides behind certainty.

Use this process:

  • Recognize a limiting thought or emotion.
  • Label it (e.g., fear, resentment, overconfidence).
  • Acknowledge it without judgment.
  • Ask:
    • “Is this helping me—or just protecting me?”
    • “What might I learn if I let go of being right?”

Final Thoughts: Letting Go of Pride, Not Self

Letting go of ego and pride isn’t about destroying yourself—it’s about becoming free.
Free to grow. Free to change. But only when you stop defending a false version of yourself.

You’re not just removing barriers—you’re rewriting what you can achieve.

“The curious paradox is that when I accept myself just as I am, then I can change.”
— Carl Rogers
