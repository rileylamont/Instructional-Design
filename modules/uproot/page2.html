Pride

Uprooting the Hidden Barriers

Welcome to the final module!

You’ve learned how to rethink limiting beliefs, regulate emotions, and remove internal distractions. Now we’re zeroing in on the bearing that’s allowed the wheel to spin in unhelpful ways—the hidden block that quietly sabotages your growth: pride.

Pride: The Liar

Here, we’re not talking about the healthy kind of pride that comes from effort or integrity. We’re talking about ego-driven pride—the kind that resists change.

It says things like:

“I already know this.”
“I’ve tried everything.”
“That’s just not who I am.”

These aren’t truths. They’re defense mechanisms—lies pride tells to protect your identity from perceived threats. But in doing so, pride locks you into old patterns and quietly sabotages growth.

Ego-driven pride is subtle. It doesn’t always shout. Sometimes it whispers. It shows up in two primary forms:

Arrogant Pride

This version says:

“I’m above this.”

Arrogant pride is fueled by a need to feel superior, right, or in control. It avoids growth not through fear, but through false certainty. It assumes it already knows, already understands, and already has it handled.

  • It’s boastful or condescending.
  • It dismisses feedback as irrelevant or uninformed.
  • It blames others instead of reflecting inward.

Arrogant pride seeks control over curiosity, prioritizing being correct over being open. It resists change because change threatens the illusion of mastery. Growth requires uncertainty, and that feels too vulnerable for an inflated ego.

Insecure Pride

This version says:

“I can’t afford to fail.”

Insecure pride is more common and often mistaken for humility or passivity. But it’s just as rooted in ego—only this time, the ego is fragile, not inflated.

  • It masks fear with avoidance, perfectionism, or detachment.
  • It avoids effort in areas where failure would feel personal.
  • It clings to identity beliefs like “I’m just not a math person,” or “That’s not me.”

Insecure pride doesn’t shout; it hides. It fears being seen as inadequate or exposed. So it builds protective walls through procrastination, self-sabotage, and limiting stories.

Target Practice

Think about your journey like aiming at a target:

  • Arrogant pride is like aiming too high;
  • Insecure pride is like aiming too low.

One isn’t better than the other—you miss the target either way!

Removing pride is simply aiming at and hitting the center.
